A two-stage early warning system that predicts Great Salt Lake dust storms days before they happen — giving families, schools, and hospitals time to prepare. It runs on free public data and a $5/month server.

Stage 1: Strategic Forecast (4–7 day window)

Three specialist models — each seeing a different line of physical evidence — combine through ensemble consensus. When two or more specialists converge, the system issues a Dust Watch with 91% recall across 40 EPA-confirmed events.

Stage 2: Tactical Monitoring (hour-level precision)

When Stage 1 fires a Watch, Stage 2 activates automatically. It monitors three realtime signals every 15 minutes — sustained NW wind, airport visibility drops, and PM10 levels — to narrow the 5-day window to the specific hours when dust will arrive.

Why two stages? Stage 1 has 91% recall but can’t tell you which day within the 5-day window. Stage 2 has hour-level precision but only works when the atmosphere is primed. Together: strategic certainty meets tactical precision. Neither alone is sufficient.

Three Specialist Models

Each specialist runs on its own server in a different data center, seeing different evidence:

Specialist 1
Hydro (Dallas)
Lake level deficit, 30-day recession rate, antecedent dryness index. “Is the lakebed exposed and dry?”
Specialist 2
Atmo (San Jose)
Dewpoint depression, minimum humidity, diurnal temperature range. “Is the atmosphere primed for mobilization?”
Specialist 3
Wind (Virginia)
NW wind persistence, peak wind speed, surface dryness. “Will wind actually transport dust to populated areas?”

Ensemble Consensus

Consensus is computed using Kuramoto coherence — a measure from physics that quantifies synchronization. When two or more specialists independently detect converging conditions, the coherence parameter rises, and the system issues a Watch.

The key insight: a windy day without lake exposure is just a windy day. A dry lakebed without NW wind produces no plume. Drying without atmospheric instability doesn’t mobilize dust. Only convergence across independent evidence lines produces events. The ensemble captures this physically.

The Great Salt Lake has lost 73% of its water since 1987. As it shrinks, 800 square miles of dry lakebed — contaminated with decades of mining runoff — is exposed to wind. The dust contains arsenic, mercury, cadmium, and selenium at concentrations above EPA safety thresholds.

A single dust event can push PM10 above 375 µg/m³ (the EPA “Hazardous” threshold is 354). The March 1, 2024 event was the most severe on record. Children walking to school, elderly on morning walks, construction workers — all exposed with zero warning.

What 5.7 days of warning means: Schools can schedule indoor recess. Hospitals can pre-position respiratory supplies. Parents can fill prescriptions. Construction can halt. Outdoor events can reschedule. The economic and health cost of an unpredicted dust storm is orders of magnitude higher than a predicted one — even if the prediction occasionally fires when the event is marginal.

The Equity Dimension

The communities closest to the lakebed — West Valley City, Magna, Kearns — are among the most socioeconomically disadvantaged in the Salt Lake metro. They have the highest asthma rates, the least access to air filtration, and the most outdoor-dependent jobs. A free, public early warning system is an environmental justice tool, not just a weather service.
