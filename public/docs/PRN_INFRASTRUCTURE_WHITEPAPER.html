Abstract

Existing public blockchains are structurally incompatible with health data regulation. Immutable ledgers cannot satisfy HIPAA right-to-amendment, GDPR right-to-erasure, or the access-control requirements of 21 CFR Part 11. We present the Paragon Resonance Network (PRN), a distributed infrastructure purpose-built for compliance-by-architecture in health AI. PRN's three contributions are: (1) Harmonic State Encoding via the General Learning Encoder (GLE) — a deterministic DCT-II pipeline producing exactly 512 bytes per biosignal regardless of duration, constituting HIPAA Safe Harbor-eligible de-identification by mathematical irreversibility; (2) Phase-Coherent Resonance (PCR) — a novel consensus primitive inspired by Kuramoto oscillator synchronization, where a 2/3-supermajority voting layer handles adversarial nodes while Kuramoto phase coherence provides a semantic validity filter native to biosignal data; and (3) a libp2p-based P2P substrate using production-proven protocols. Empirical benchmarks on 30 PhysioNet records (BIDMC + MIT-BIH, March 2026) confirm GLE's 125:1 primary compression is exact and deterministic within a canonical implementation. Secondary harmonic compression achieves 14.6:1 additional reduction at 51.9% cosine fidelity (N=7, 95% CI: 34.2–77.9%, breathing). At network scale, GLE's fixed-output property means 1 billion users' health fingerprints constitute 512 GB of payload — a single commodity disk. Two nodes are currently live on a Fly.io bootstrap cluster.

Keywords: Distributed Health Infrastructure, HIPAA Compliance, Blockchain, Harmonic State Encoding, Phase-Coherent Resonance, Kuramoto Synchronization, General Learning Encoder, Privacy-Preserving Computation

1. The Compliance Imperative

1.1 Why Existing Blockchains Cannot Serve Health Data

Health data is subject to the most stringent privacy regulations in existence. Any infrastructure that claims to support health AI must first demonstrate legal capability to hold the data. For all major public blockchains, this capability is absent — not as a product limitation but as an architectural one.

HIPAA (45 CFR §164.308, United States). Protected Health Information (PHI) written to a public blockchain is permanently and irrevocably disclosed. The HIPAA minimum-necessary standard is violated upon the first write. The Right to Amendment (45 CFR §164.526) requires that incorrect health records be correctable — architecturally impossible on an immutable ledger. The Accounting of Disclosures requirement (45 CFR §164.528) cannot be satisfied when data is readable by any network participant.

GDPR Article 17 — Right to Erasure (EU). The “right to be forgotten” is a legal requirement for any processor of EU residents' biometric data. No transaction written to the Ethereum, Solana, or Bitcoin mainchain has ever been erased. Any operator storing biometric data on a public chain is in per-record violation across all EU jurisdictions.

GDPR Article 9 — Special Categories. Biometric data is explicitly enumerated as a special category requiring explicit consent, a Data Protection Impact Assessment, and stricter storage limitations than standard personal data. Public blockchains have no access controls. Structural non-compliance is unavoidable.

21 CFR Part 11 — FDA Electronic Records (US). Clinical data submitted to the FDA must be stored with audit trails, data integrity verification, and access controls. Public blockchains satisfy the first two requirements but are definitionally incapable of per-record access controls. PRN satisfies all three: the blockchain ledger provides immutable audit trails; GLE's deterministic pipeline provides verifiable data integrity; and the on-device encoding architecture ensures that only authorized coefficient representations enter the network.

3. GLE Harmonic State Encoding

3.1 The GLE Pipeline

The General Learning Encoder (GLE) [Tran & Tran, 2026] transforms a raw biosignal audio stream into a fixed-size 128-coefficient representation. The pipeline is deterministic and subject-invariant: the same mathematical operation processes any biological signal regardless of modality or subject identity. On the EEG Foundation Challenge 2025 (NeurIPS workshop, 2,000+ teams), GLE achieved 13.5× the improvement over baseline on subject-invariant representation, providing empirical evidence for this property.

The canonical reference implementation is gle-encoder.js (paragondao-landing/src/agent/). All PRN nodes must use this implementation or a bit-compatible equivalent to ensure cross-node reproducibility (see Section 3.3).

3.2 Mathematical Formulation

Given a 1-second audio segment \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{16000}\), GLE produces a 128-dimensional coefficient vector \(\mathbf{g} \in \mathbb{R}^{128}\) through the following stages.

Stage 1 — Normalization

(1) $$\hat{x}[n] = \frac{x[n]}{\max_n |x[n]|}, \quad n = 0, \ldots, 15999$$

Stage 2 — Downsampling to DCT Window

Linear interpolation resamples \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) from 16,000 samples to \(W = 2048\) samples (the DCT window size).

Stage 3 — Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT-II)

(2) $$X[k] = \alpha(k) \sum_{n=0}^{W-1} \hat{x}[n] \cos\!\left(\frac{\pi k(2n+1)}{2W}\right), \quad k = 0, \ldots, W-1$$

where \(\alpha(k) = \sqrt{1/W}\) for \(k=0\) and \(\sqrt{2/W}\) for \(k > 0\) (orthonormal scaling).

Stage 4 — Identity Band Extraction

Coefficients corresponding to 20–1000 Hz are retained and linearly resized to 128 dimensions, yielding \(\mathbf{g} \in \mathbb{R}^{128}\). For multi-window audio, 1-second windows are processed individually and averaged component-wise.

3.3 Primary Compression: Mathematical Property

The 125:1 primary compression ratio is a mathematical consequence of the pipeline, not an empirical estimate:

(3) $$\text{ratio} = \frac{1 \text{ s} \times 16{,}000 \text{ samples} \times 4 \text{ bytes}}{128 \times 4 \text{ bytes}} = \frac{64{,}000}{512} = 125\text{:}1 \text{ (exact)}$$

This ratio is identical for a 1-second clip and an 8-hour sleep recording. No confidence interval is applicable; it is deterministic.

Cross-platform reproducibility caveat. DCT-II computed in IEEE 754 float32 is not guaranteed to be bit-for-bit identical across different hardware architectures, SIMD implementations, or numerical libraries. Python scipy.fft.dct and JavaScript WebAudio will differ at the 5th–8th decimal place due to floating-point rounding. To ensure consensus on identical GLE outputs, all PRN nodes must use the canonical reference implementation (gle-encoder.js) or a verified bit-compatible port. Cross-platform reproducibility testing is an active engineering requirement prior to multi-operator deployment.

3.4 Theoretical Foundation: Compressed Sensing

The GLE's effectiveness rests on a well-established mathematical result. Biological signals exhibit \(1/f^\alpha\) power-law spectral structure (Goldberger et al., 2002; Peng et al., 1994), meaning they are approximately K-sparse in the frequency domain. Compressed sensing theory (Donoho, 2006; Candès, Romberg & Tao, 2006) guarantees exact sparse signal recovery when the measurement matrix satisfies the Restricted Isometry Property (RIP):

(4) $$(1 - \delta_s)\|\mathbf{x}\|_2^2 \;\leq\; \|\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}\|_2^2 \;\leq\; (1 + \delta_s)\|\mathbf{x}\|_2^2$$

If \(\delta_{2s} < \sqrt{2} - 1 \approx 0.414\), \(\ell_1\) minimization exactly recovers any \(s\)-sparse signal. The DCT-II is a standard incoherent basis used in compressed sensing applications. David Donoho received the Gauss Prize (International Mathematical Union, 2018) for foundational contributions to this theory.

The closest engineering precedent for high-ratio compression without accuracy loss is Deep Compression [Han, Mao & Dally, ICLR 2016 Best Paper Award]: 35× on AlexNet and 49× on VGG-16 with zero accuracy degradation. GLE's 125:1 primary ratio is in this lineage applied to biosignal inputs.

3.6 Privacy by Mathematical Construction

The GLE encoding is irreversible. The band extraction step — retaining 20–1000 Hz of a 0–8000 Hz spectrum and resizing to 128 dimensions — is a lossy projection. No decompression algorithm exists because there is no compressed representation: only a different mathematical space. The original audio waveform cannot be reconstructed from the 128 coefficients.

This irreversibility is architecturally stronger than encryption. Encrypted data can be decrypted if a key is compromised. GLE-encoded data cannot be “decrypted” because no key exists. The privacy guarantee is permanent and independent of future cryptanalytic advances. This is the mathematical basis for PRN's HIPAA compliance architecture.

4. Phase-Coherent Resonance Consensus

4.1 Why Existing Consensus Mechanisms Are Semantically Wrong for Health Data

Bitcoin's Proof of Work answers: “Who expends the most energy?” Ethereum's Gasper answers: “Who has staked the most capital?” Tendermint BFT answers: “Do 2/3 of validators agree?”

All three are questions about scarcity or vote count. None are questions about the biological plausibility of the data being admitted. For a health data network, this matters: a fabricated coefficient vector — one with no plausible biological origin — would pass any capital-based or vote-based consensus mechanism trivially.

PRN's Phase-Coherent Resonance addresses a different question: is this incoming state transition phase-coherent with what the network currently knows about biosignal dynamics? This makes the consensus mechanism semantically native to its data type.

4.2 Kuramoto Oscillator Foundation

Kuramoto (1975) described a model of coupled phase oscillators that synchronize spontaneously when coupling exceeds a critical threshold:

(5) $$\frac{d\theta_i}{dt} = \omega_i + \frac{K}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} \sin(\theta_j - \theta_i)$$

where \(\theta_i\) is the phase of oscillator \(i\), \(\omega_i\) its natural frequency, and \(K\) the coupling strength. The network order parameter \(r = |\sum_j e^{i\theta_j}| / N\) measures global coherence: \(r \to 1\) (full synchronization) when \(K > K_c\) (critical coupling), \(r \approx 0\) (incoherence) otherwise. Strogatz (2000) and Acebrón et al. (2005) provide formal convergence analysis. Okamoto et al. (2014) demonstrated application to distributed constraint optimization in multi-agent systems.

4.3 PCR Phase State: Formal Specification

Each PRN node \(i\) maintains a Kuramoto-style oscillator state \((\omega_i, \theta_i)\):

  • Natural frequency \(\omega_i\): Derived from the node's enrolled GLE coefficient distribution — specifically, the dominant spectral frequency of the node's baseline coefficient vector. This ties each node's oscillator to the biosignal statistics of its enrolled population.
  • Phase \(\theta_i(t)\): Updated continuously by the Kuramoto equation above, using the phases broadcast by peers. Phase state is gossiped among connected peers on the /paragon/phase-sync/1.0.0 protocol at a configurable heartbeat interval.
  • Network aggregate phase \(\Theta(t)\): The circular mean of all known peer phases: \(\Theta = \text{arg}\!\left(\sum_j e^{i\theta_j}\right)\). Each validator independently computes \(\Theta\) from its local view of peer phases received within the last block window.

Partition behavior. Under network partition, each partition independently maintains its own \(\Theta\). When the partition heals, phases re-synchronize via the Kuramoto coupling term. Blocks produced during partition are accepted if they satisfy the BFT voting layer (Section 4.4) and their phase state falls within \(\text{PHASE\_TOLERANCE}\) of the post-merge \(\Theta\). Conflicting blocks from both sides of a partition are resolved by the BFT supermajority rule; PCR phase coherence acts as an additional admission filter but does not override BFT for finality.

4.4 Block Validity: Dual-Layer Protocol

PCR uses two independent layers for block admission:

Layer 1 — Semantic Filter (Kuramoto phase coherence). For each transaction in a proposed block, the validator computes the cosine similarity between the transaction's embedded harmonic state and the validator's current phase vector:

(6) $$\text{admit}(\text{tx}) = \mathbf{1}\!\left[\,\left|\theta_{\text{tx}} - \Theta\right| < \delta_\phi\right]$$

where \(\delta_\phi = 0.1\) radians is the phase tolerance and \(\theta_{\text{tx}}\) is the phase angle of the transaction's harmonic state vector (computed via PhaseMatcher in 10 mHz frequency buckets). A transaction fails Layer 1 if its coefficient vector is anomalous relative to the network's current oscillator state.

Layer 2 — Adversarial Safety (2/3 BFT supermajority). A block is valid if \(\geq 2/3\) of validators vote to accept it. A validator votes YES if and only if \(\geq 2/3\) of the block's transactions pass Layer 1. This preserves standard BFT safety guarantees for adversarial nodes.

The two layers are independent. Layer 1 (Kuramoto) handles semantic plausibility for honest-but-heterogeneous nodes. Layer 2 (BFT) handles adversarial nodes regardless of their phase behavior. A malicious node generating perfectly phase-coherent but fabricated data is caught by Layer 2. A misbehaving honest node broadcasting biologically implausible data is caught by Layer 1.

4.6 Liveness Analysis

PRN inherits liveness from its BFT voting layer: progress is guaranteed as long as \(\geq 2/3\) of validators are online and honest, matching standard Tendermint liveness conditions. The Kuramoto layer introduces a potential liveness risk: if the network's aggregate phase shifts rapidly (e.g., during a large-scale respiratory event affecting 500M enrolled users), valid transactions might transiently fail Layer 1.

This is mitigated by three design choices: (i) \(\delta_\phi\) is set conservatively at 0.1 radians (\(\approx 5.7°\)), wide enough to accommodate normal population variance; (ii) the block time in PERFORMANCE mode (1 second) allows rapid phase re-synchronization before the next block; (iii) if a node's local \(\Theta\) is stale (last peer heartbeat > 3 block times), it falls back to BFT-only validation until phase state is refreshed. Formal liveness bounds for the Kuramoto layer under adversarial phase injection remain an open research direction.

What PCR establishes: PCR is the first consensus primitive that is semantically native to biosignal data. Kuramoto dynamics provide natural distributed coordination for honest heterogeneous nodes. The 2/3-supermajority layer handles adversarial nodes independently.

What remains open research: Formal Byzantine fault tolerance via Kuramoto dynamics alone has not been established in the literature. No existing paper directly proves that Kuramoto phase synchronization is sufficient to exclude Byzantine adversaries without an independent voting layer. The claim “PCR is BFT-secure” must be qualified: PRN's adversarial resilience derives from the BFT voting layer; PCR adds a biologically-grounded semantic filter on top. Fusing Kuramoto dynamics with formal BFT proofs is an active research direction we are pursuing.

5. Threat Model

5.1 Assumptions

PRN assumes a partial synchrony network model (messages are eventually delivered with unknown but bounded delay) and a Byzantine adversary controlling fewer than 1/3 of validators by stake-weight. Standard BFT conditions apply: safety is guaranteed if <1/3 validators are Byzantine; liveness requires >2/3 to be online and honest.

7. Stack Integration

7.1 Relationship to HFTP

HFTP (Harmonic Frequency Transfer Protocol) is the AI-native agent communication protocol for frequency coefficient exchange. PRN provides the trust substrate: when two HFTP agents exchange GLE coefficient vectors, PRN's PCR consensus verifies that the coefficients were legitimately generated before the exchange is recorded on-chain. HFTP handles what travels between agents; PRN handles whether that exchange is trusted by the network.

7.2 Relationship to HF-Auth

HF-Auth provides five-layer local authorization and biometric binding entirely on-device. PRN operates entirely off-device. No biometric data crosses the boundary between the two layers. HF-Auth gates the GLE encoding event; PRN records its proof. The security model is defense-in-depth: HF-Auth protects the device side, PRN protects the network side.

8.1 The Structural Insight

Satoshi Nakamoto's Bitcoin whitepaper (2008) contained one sentence that changed finance: “A purely peer-to-peer version of electronic cash would allow online payments to be sent directly from one party to another without going through a financial institution.” The insight was that trust could be mathematical rather than institutional.

PRN's equivalent: “A person's health can be known by AI without that person's health data leaving their device.” The same structural insight applied to health: remove the trusted intermediary (hospital, EHR vendor, health data broker), replace institutional trust with mathematical structure (GLE irreversibility + PRN consensus).

If a 30-second breathing pattern can be encoded into 512 bytes that cannot be reverse-engineered, and if that encoding generalizes across individuals never seen in training, then health records as we know them — files, databases, PHI repositories — become unnecessary for most AI health applications. The Paragon whitepaper's purpose is to make that question thinkable for the first time.
